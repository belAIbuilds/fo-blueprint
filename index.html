Core Investment Philosophy

The investment philosophy is the intellectual DNA of the family office. It is the set of beliefs — about markets, returns, risk, and human behavior — from which all allocation decisions flow. This philosophy draws from three traditions: the endowment model (Yale/Harvard), conviction-based venture capital (Benchmark, Founders Fund), and technology-operator investing (a16z, Thrive Capital).

Belief 1: The Illiquidity Premium is Real and Persistent

Capital that can tolerate illiquidity earns a structural premium of 300–500 basis points annually over liquid alternatives. As permanent family capital without redemption obligations or fund lifecycle constraints, we can capture this premium more aggressively than endowments (which face spending mandates) and far more than pensions (which face actuarial constraints). The illiquidity premium is our single largest structural advantage.

Belief 2: Technology-Driven Power Laws Dominate Returns

In venture capital, 6% of deals produce 60% of aggregate returns. In public technology equities, a handful of companies account for the vast majority of index outperformance over any decade. This demands a portfolio construction philosophy that maximizes exposure to the right tail of the distribution — not one that minimizes variance. We would rather own one Nvidia than fifty average companies.

Belief 3: Information Advantage is Compounding and Defensible

Superior returns in technology investing originate from proprietary information loops: seeing deals earlier (sourcing advantage), understanding technology deeper (analytical advantage), and acting faster (decision advantage). These advantages compound — each investment generates network effects that improve the next investment. An AI-native operating model accelerates this compounding by orders of magnitude.

Belief 4: Founder Quality is the Dominant Variable

In early-stage technology investing, the founding team explains more variance in outcomes than market size, product-market fit, or competitive dynamics. Markets can be created, products can be pivoted, competition can be outmaneuvered — but founder quality is largely fixed at the point of investment. This belief concentrates our analytical effort on founder assessment.

Belief 5: Active Ownership Creates Value

Capital alone is a commodity. Smart capital — defined as capital combined with operational insight, network access, and strategic guidance — commands better deal access, better terms, and better outcomes. We invest not only money but also expertise, relationships, and infrastructure into every portfolio company.

We willingly accept short-term volatility and mark-to-market fluctuations in exchange for higher long-term compounding. What we will not accept is the risk of irreversible loss.

Liquidity Strategy

Liquidity management is the structural foundation that enables the aggressive illiquidity tilt. Without disciplined liquidity management, a 60% illiquid allocation becomes a vulnerability rather than an advantage. The strategy operates on three tiers.

Capital Call Forecasting

All uncalled commitments to VC funds and PE funds are modeled with probabilistic drawdown schedules. The AI system maintains a rolling 12-month capital call forecast that is updated weekly. This forecast directly informs the size of Tier 1 and Tier 2 reserves. The system flags when projected capital calls would breach the liquidity floor, enabling proactive de-risking of the liquid sleeve before stress materializes.

Stress Scenario

Worst-case test: Simultaneous 30% drawdown in public markets + acceleration of all outstanding capital calls + zero distributions from VC/PE portfolio + 12-month freeze in exit markets. Under this scenario, the three-tier structure must maintain positive cash flow for 18 months without any forced liquidation of illiquid positions. The model is stress-tested quarterly.

Capital Preservation Principles

Real Wealth Preservation

The minimum acceptable outcome is the preservation of purchasing power after inflation. Net real returns must exceed 0% in any rolling 5-year period. Nominal returns that fail to beat EUR inflation are treated as losses.

No scenario should produce a total portfolio drawdown exceeding 35%. If stress tests indicate this threshold is at risk, the liquid sleeve allocation must be increased until the limit is restored.

Anti-Fragility

The portfolio should benefit from market dislocations. Cash reserves and liquid positions provide dry powder to invest aggressively when valuations compress — turning crises into opportunities.

Human vs. AI Responsibilities

The most critical design decision in an AI-first organization is defining the boundary between human and machine responsibilities. The guiding principle: AI handles scale, speed, and consistency. Humans handle judgment, relationships, and conviction.

Responsibility Matrix

The Golden Rule: If the consequence of an AI error is reversible (wrong screening score → human catches it in review), automate aggressively. If the consequence is irreversible (deploying capital, damaging a relationship, missing a critical risk), keep humans in the loop with veto authority. The cost of false positives (AI flags something incorrectly) should be wasted human time reviewing. The cost of false negatives (AI misses something) should never be lost capital.

AI Maturity and Human Evolution

As the AI systems mature from V1 to V3, the human team's role evolves — not diminishes. In Year 1, humans spend 60% of their time on tasks that AI will eventually handle (data gathering, report compilation, basic research). By Year 3, humans spend 80% of their time on irreplaceable activities: relationship building, founder assessment, strategic thinking, deal negotiation, and portfolio company coaching. AI does not reduce the need for talent — it concentrates talent on the activities that matter most.

Decision Quality Framework

Exit Readiness Assessment

Annual Exit Readiness Check (every portfolio company, starting Year 3): (1) Exit comparables: 3+ comparable exits at >5x revenue in trailing 12 months? (2) Company readiness: clean financials, strong management team, growth trajectory? (3) Market timing: favorable M&A/IPO conditions? (4) FO objectives: return target achievable at current trajectory? (5) Founder alignment: founder ready and willing to discuss exit options? If 4+ of 5 criteria are met → initiate active exit process.

Post-Mortem Protocol

Every exit (successful or write-off) triggers a mandatory post-mortem analysis. The post-mortem documents: what the original thesis was, whether the thesis proved correct, what signals were missed, what the FO did well and could improve, and lessons for future investments. Post-mortems are stored in the Knowledge System and used to calibrate the screening model. This is the primary mechanism for institutional learning.
